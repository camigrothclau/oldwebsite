APP MENU STRUCTURE: A/B USABILITY TEST

PROJECT BACKGROUND

At this time I was a project lead at EyeReply conducting several UX Research studies for different clients. One client of retail and supermarket business asked me and my team to test the app communication of their members app based on two different menu structures. The client’s challenge was to both meet business needs and at the same time serve a satisfying user experience through the app design. They had a couple of design ideas of which the menu structure differed from one another. We decided on two versions to test with users.

WHAT I DID

I recruited 10 participants based on target group criteria I had agreed on with the client. I tested the app menu concepts by conducting an A/B approach, rotating the two versions from test to test (A-B, B-A...) in a UX lab through in depth interview, observation and mobile eye tracking to catch the visual attention and user behaviour and to see whether users would interact with different elements based on the design of menus. The eye tracking data could tell, if participants were reading relevant information. Eye tracking could also tell which elements were too hidden or tell why users would interact in a certain way or why they understood the information in certain ways. I gave the participants three tasks and observed them solving the tasks: ‘check your bonus’, ‘imagine that you should go pay for groceries with your bonus’ and ‘try to find products with discount in the app that is interesting for you’. In the end I sat down with the client team to discuss findings and actionable insights.

PRODUCT OR CONCEPT

App for paying at the supermarket and to earn points when buying groceries.

LOCATION

In a UX Lab in Copenhagen with mobile eye tracking glasses.

PARTICIPANTS

10 participants:
Current members of the organisation.
50% uses the app,
50% men 50% women,
Age: 25-55

MY ROLE

Consultant: Recruiter,
Research Planner,
Interviewer,
Report assistant

DURATION

1 Interview: 40 min
All interviews: 3 days
In total: 1 month

IN-STORE PRODUCT, EYE TRACKING RESEARCH

PROJECT BACKGROUND

During my time working as a research consultant a big client selling smoking cessation products asked me and my team to examine their position on the market in relation to one big competitor. They also wanted to know how consumers perceived their product on the shelf and how marketing initiatives such as small samples of gum, lozenges etc performed visually on the desk. They further wanted to learn more about consumer product preferences in general as well as consumer experience of smoking cessation packaging

WHAT I DID

I was working with my boss and one other researcher for three weeks to plan the research methods in order to be able to provide our client with valuable insights matching their needs. We decided to examine the actual in-store purchase behaviour on location and conduct individual interviews about the purchase experience and product preferences. I was in charge of recruiting the participants and I completed the interviews and observations. All 40 participants were asked to go into the store to buy a smoking cessation product of their own choice and I observed the behaviour in 4 different stores to see differences in behaviour and patterns based on environments: where in the store would they look for the product? Did they ask the shop assistents about anything? Did they spot the marketing initiatives in the store? Did they act differently based on environments? Did they choose our clients product or the one of the competitor? I held semi-structured individual interviews right after the purchase asking why they chose the product in terms of preferences and considerations of the competitive product. Finally I interviewed the participants about their associations around the packaging. After three weeks of research I wrote a report about behaviour patterns and insights about preferences and consumer needs from our study and presented it to our client to discuss next steps with them.

PRODUCT OR CONCEPT

Smoking cessation products: gums, inhalators, lozenges or patches.

LOCATION

4 physical stores: 1 pharmacy, 1 beauty shop, 1 kiosk, 1 supermarket

PARTICIPANTS

40 participants: Smoking cessation product consumers, daily or occasionally. Spread out on age and gender.

MY ROLE

Consultant: Recruitment Lead, Planner, Interviewer, Observer, Analyzer & Reporter

DURATION

1 Interview: 45 min
All interviews: 3 weeks
In total: 2 months

WORKSHOPS FOR TEENS WITH DIABETES

PROJECT BACKGROUND

I always found it interesting to examine how products serve important purposes and needs to specific target groups. This interest led me in 2016 to research the daily lives of teens with diabetes and the tools they use everyday to detect a gap of (perhaps) something missing to make their daily lives even easier. For this project I wanted to examine whether a Gamification strategy could support teens between the age of 13 and 17 with type 1 diabetes to become motivated to overcome everyday challenges related to regulating their blood glucose – particularly among friends and peers outside home.

WHAT I DID

To examine the problem, I conducted two workshops through a participatory design approach with nine teens with diabetes type 1. The outcomes of the first workshop were analyzed to detect how diabetes-related tools support mediating diabetes-related everyday practices in relation to regulate blood glucose. The outcomes of the second workshop were as well analyzed to detect how game elements can mediate certain intended behaviors for the teens to become motivated to overcome five main challenges that I found in the first workshop. Secondly, the outcomes of the second workshop were analyzed by applying a gamification theory as a research tool to investigate the effects of game elements for diabetes practices. Through a self- determination theory, I analyzed outcomes to detect how the teens could be motivated by the suggested game elements for overcoming each of the five everyday challenge themes.

PRODUCT OR CONCEPT

Gamification concept for motivating teens with diabetes to control their disease.

LOCATION

At a social cafe for teens with diabetes

PARTICIPANTS

9 participants: teens with diabetes in the age of 13-17 years

MY ROLE

Workshop Facilitator, Recruiter, Candidate Student

DURATION

1 workshop: 90 min
2 workshops in total
Time in total: 4 months

CITIZEN JOURNEYS IN MUNICIPALITY SYSTEMS

PROJECT BACKGROUND

The Copenhagen Municipality came to us (research consultant agency, EyeReply) as they were experiencing a lot of frustration and confusion among citizens applying for monetary benefits. Citizens have to visit a number og touch points to receive the information the need for applying correctly. They have to fill out an online form to apply, but what many citizens do not know is, that they have to talk to a number of social workers before applying online.

WHAT I DID

I visited three different job centers to conduct contextual research. I met up with 9 social workers in different departments. My plan with this contextual research was to detect challenges in their everyday supporting citizens in applying for monetary benefits. Which challenges did they experience? What would they typically have to help citizens with and how? What information material could they provide the citizens with? I sought to answer such questions. At job centers I also interviewed 9 people applying for monetary benefits in order to cover challenges from their point of view. I further did a Usability test of the online form with 9 participants strategically recruited for applying for monetary benefits. Users would often not be able to fill the form without having visited several other touch points in advance. After gathering all the research I could clearly see challenges throughout the journey. I suggested new ways of providing information to citizens before, during and after applying for monetary benefits. I further shared insights about social workers’ challenges caused by not collaborating in helping citizens on their journey.

PRODUCT OR CONCEPT

User Journey in Municipality for applying for monetary benefits.

LOCATION

In 3 different job centers (social workers and visitors) and in a UX lab (9 applicants)

PARTICIPANTS

9 Social workers, 9 job center visitors, 9 potential applicants:(3 no-danish-speaking,3 students and 3 with special benefits)

MY ROLE

UX Consultant: Recruitment Lead, Research Planner, Interviewer, Report assistant

DURATION

1 Interview: 40 min
All interviews: 3 days
In total: 1 month

IN-FIELD (GAS STATION) UX APP RESEARCH

PROJECT BACKGROUND

One of the first research projects I took part in at EyeReply - a research agency - took place at a gas station during the coldest of the coldest winter days 2015-2016. We drove out three people - my boss, a researcher and me - to conduct 20 tests of an app from a big insurance company, who wanted to know whether their app design and idea was compelling and easy to understand and use. They had build a high-fi prototype ready to test.

WHAT I DID

My boss and I collaborated on planning the research design and interview guides and I recruited participants with help from two other half time recruiters. We tested all touch points in the app during the study: on-boarding, usability at the gas station and login/user profile. Participants were told to arrive by car. First I held small pre interviews in the car. Afterwards participants were told to use the app to receive their discount (as if they were fueling). Participants were told to rate their experience across several parametres e.g. navigation, information, ease of use, naming of icons, overview, support etc. to determine which parts of the app needed to be prioritized first. We ended up presenting our findings to the client and discussed actionable insights that they should continue to focus on next.

PRODUCT OR CONCEPT

App from Insurance company providing users with a discount on gas stations when fueling.

LOCATION

At a gas station in Copenhagen, Denmark.

PARTICIPANTS

20 participants: 10 current insurance customers and 10 customers of other insurance company.
Age: 18-65

MY ROLE

UX Consultant: Recruitment, Interview assistant, Note taker, Report assistant

DURATION

1 Interview: 45 min
All interviews: 8 days
In total: 1,5 months
